Building a profitable solo app and website studio
Position the business narrowly
The strongest version of your business is not “I build apps for anyone.” It is a narrower, easier-to-buy offer: a solo studio that builds websites, lightweight web apps, client portals, and internal tools for a defined type of buyer. The U.S. Small Business Administration recommends that small businesses define a specific target market, competitive advantage, pricing context, and sales plan; applied to your situation, that means choosing one or two buyer types and one or two outcomes you repeatedly solve, instead of presenting yourself as a generalist coder. The market backdrop is favorable: the Bureau of Labor Statistics projects faster-than-average growth for both software developers and web developers, which supports a focused service business around digital build work. 

Given that you are solo and already have people asking for websites, the most bankable launch model is: websites first, web apps second, open-ended custom app consulting third. In practice, that means you lead with high-clarity offers such as “service-business websites,” “client portals,” “booking and intake systems,” “member dashboards,” or “internal workflow tools,” and you treat larger or fuzzier app ideas as paid discovery before you quote the build. That structure reduces sales friction, makes scoping easier, and gives you an entry-level offer that can expand into higher-margin app work later. This is an inference from the SBA’s guidance on target markets and competitive advantage, combined with the fact that your first warm demand is already website work. 

A smart solo business model usually has three revenue streams. The first is paid discovery, where you turn vague ideas into scope, sitemap, technical recommendation, timeline, and quote. The second is build revenue, where you sell fixed-scope websites or milestone-based web-app projects. The third is ongoing support, where you offer maintenance, edits, bug fixes, hosting/vendor coordination, and small enhancements on a monthly retainer. That mix matters because project work creates cash now, while retainers smooth income and reduce the feast-or-famine cycle that hurts solo operators. Phased project structures and stage-based payments are also consistent with standard software development agreement advice. 

Build your website as a sales asset
Your own website should not behave like a passive portfolio. It should behave like your first product: clear, fast, simple, mobile-friendly, and focused on getting qualified leads to contact you. Nielsen Norman Group stresses that portfolios should tell a story, be easy to navigate, keep content as the focus rather than flashy presentation, and show the process behind the work rather than only polished screenshots. 

A strong solo studio website usually has six pages or sections. Home should say who you help, what you build, and what outcome clients get. Services should package your offers into understandable buying choices. Work should contain a dedicated case-studies page, not just a gallery. Process should explain how projects move from discovery to launch. About should make you credible and personal. Contact should make the next step obvious and low-friction. HubSpot specifically recommends a dedicated case-studies page, prominent proof on the homepage, and case studies that clearly show the initial challenge, goals, process, and results. 

Your case studies matter more than your tech stack list. The case-study format that works best is: problem, your role, constraints, approach, screenshots or demos, and results or lessons learned. NN/g recommends choosing a few detailed case studies over a large quantity of shallow examples, and it specifically advises making them scannable, process-driven, and story-based. If a project is under NDA, you can still show sanitized process artifacts, wireframes, blurred screenshots, or recreated generic visuals. 

Because some of your projects are still in progress, you do not need to wait for perfect final launches to publish proof. You can publish them as “beta,” “in progress,” or “concept build,” as long as you are honest about status and what results, if any, are still pending. What matters is showing that you can take a problem from idea to working software. Your GitHub links can support this, but they should be secondary to a short, client-friendly explanation of the business problem and your solution. NN/g’s guidance is clear that final screenshots only tell part of the story; buyers want to understand how you think and work. 

Your contact page should do more than show a blank form. It should give visitors at least two ways to reach you and tell them what happens next. Good contact pages route people clearly, offer multiple contact methods, and reduce the feeling that a form is a black box. For a solo studio, that usually means: a short inquiry form, a visible email address, and an optional discovery-call link. You can also include a short promise such as “I reply within one business day” if you can actually meet it. 

As soon as your site collects inquiries, analytics, or newsletter signups, treat privacy and security statements seriously. The Federal Trade Commission has repeatedly emphasized that if a business collects and retains data, it must protect it, keep only what it needs, and avoid deceptive privacy or security claims. Put plainly: only ask for the information you really need on your contact form, and do not promise “enterprise-grade security” or “fully compliant” anything unless you can back it up. 

Use a small set of high-trust marketing and contact channels
Your best early marketing is proof plus responsiveness, not broad paid advertising. The SBA’s marketing guidance says a marketing plan should identify your target market, competitive advantage, sales method, goals, action plan, and budget. For you, that means choosing a handful of channels you can actually maintain rather than trying to be everywhere at once. 

The first channel is referrals and warm introductions. For a solo studio, referrals are usually the highest-trust, lowest-friction source of work. The SBA’s local-marketing guidance recommends following up with customers, asking for testimonials, participating in professional groups, listing in relevant directories, and building referral mechanisms. Once you finish your first paid projects, a simple follow-up asking for a review or testimonial is worth more than most cold outreach. 

The second channel is local search through a verified Google Business Profile if you plan to work with nearby companies. Google says businesses with complete and accurate information are more likely to appear in local results, verification helps visibility, and local ranking is driven mainly by relevance, distance, and popularity. If you work from home and do not want your address public, Google allows service-area businesses to hide the address and define service areas instead. Your profile can include your website, phone number, hours, reviews, services offered, and more. 

The third channel is a LinkedIn Service Page tied to your personal profile. LinkedIn says a Service Page is a dedicated landing page that showcases services and operates on a request-and-proposal model, with free client contact regardless of connection degree. Clients can also leave reviews, and LinkedIn displays responsiveness signals if you typically reply within a day or respond to at least 80% of inquiries. For a solo consultant or studio, this is a practical way to add trust signals before your own site has a large testimonial base. 

The fourth channel is selective local and niche networking. The SBA recommends managing your listings, joining professional groups, participating in local business communities, and using relevant directories. For your business, that could mean local chambers, coworking communities, startup meetups, industry-specific groups, or local service-business circles where owners often need websites, booking systems, dashboards, or workflow fixes but do not want to hire a full agency. 

The fifth channel is content built from your case studies. HubSpot’s guidance is straightforward: case studies are valuable proof assets, belong on a dedicated page, can support the homepage, and can be reused across social posts, email, and sales follow-up. That means every finished project should eventually turn into one website case study, one short social post, one testimonial quote, and one simple follow-up asset you can send to prospects during sales conversations. 

Run client work with strong scope, contract, and security discipline
The biggest operational risk in your business will not be writing code. It will be managing expectations. The most common problems in small software projects are vague requirements, late clarifications, uncontrolled change requests, slow client feedback, timeline drift, payment arguments, and ownership confusion. The Project Management Institute notes that poor understanding of customer requirements before project scope definition and contract signing is a major source of scope creep, and it recommends reviewing requirements carefully, defining customer roles, and creating a process for out-of-scope work. 

In practice, that means every client project should have a simple contract stack: a proposal, a service agreement or master agreement, a statement of work, milestone payments, and a change-order process. Your statement of work should define deliverables, what is explicitly out of scope, client responsibilities, content dependencies, platforms, revision limits, and acceptance criteria. PMI’s guidance is particularly strong on this point: vague statements help no one, and change requests should be evaluated and agreed through a defined process rather than absorbed informally. 

For payment structure, avoid a single all-at-once fee due at the end. Justia’s overview of software development agreements notes that phased timelines and payments help both sides: the client gets progress visibility and approval points, while the developer gets compensation as work is completed and protection if the project falls apart midstream. For a solo shop, milestone billing is not just good hygiene; it is cash-flow protection. 

You also need to decide and document ownership clearly. The U.S. Copyright Office makes two points that matter here. First, copyright initially vests in the author. Second, a transfer of copyright ownership generally is not valid unless it is in writing and signed by the rights owner. Justia’s software-agreement guidance adds the practical layer: unless you expressly transfer ownership, the developer may own the work; and if you use background technology, templates, or reusable code, your agreement should say you retain those rights while the client receives the needed license. 

That leads to a sensible default position for a solo studio: the client gets ownership of the custom final deliverables upon final payment, but you retain your pre-existing code libraries, templates, frameworks, workflows, and generalized know-how. This protects your ability to reuse what makes your small business efficient, while still giving the client what they are buying. If you want to showcase the project publicly later, say so in the contract and reserve a limited portfolio-display right, subject to confidentiality. 

Support and warranty need clear limits too. Justia notes that software agreements commonly include a defined warranty period and warns against indefinite warranties. For a solo operator, the cleanest setup is a defined post-launch bug-fix window for issues that make the deliverable fail to meet the agreed specification, followed by paid maintenance for new requests, enhancements, and vendor changes. That distinction keeps post-launch work from quietly turning into open-ended free labor. 

On security and privacy, build stricter habits early. The National Institute of Standards and Technology recommends secure software development practices through its Secure Software Development Framework, including preparing the organization, protecting software, producing well-secured releases, and responding to vulnerabilities. The FTC’s app and business guidance reinforces the same principles in plain English: collect only the data you need, limit access, protect what you keep, and do not make misleading claims about privacy or security. Even for a tiny studio, these are client-facing differentiators. 

Structure pricing for profit instead of guesswork
Your pricing should be designed around profitability, not around what feels “fair” in the moment. The SBA’s pricing guidance emphasizes calculating costs, separating direct and indirect expenses, researching competition, and using pricing models that both cover your costs and reflect delivered value. That means your prices need to pay for more than coding time: they also need to cover sales time, scoping, revisions, admin, software subscriptions, taxes, insurance, and the risk you absorb as the one-person business. 

A useful pricing architecture for your business is this. Use paid discovery for uncertainty. Use fixed pricing for well-bounded websites. Use milestone pricing for custom web-app builds. Use monthly retainers for support. Use hourly billing only for small out-of-scope requests or advisory work. That structure aligns price with risk. It also keeps you from giving away consulting during sales calls and from turning unclear requests into low-margin projects. Stage-based development agreements and phased payments are well aligned with this model. 

You should also set your rate floor higher than employee thinking would suggest. The BLS reports median annual wages of $133,080 for software developers and $90,930 for web developers in May 2024. At the same time, the Internal Revenue Service says self-employment tax is 15.3% and estimated taxes often must be paid quarterly. In other words, if you charge as though every hour were salaried employee time, you will underprice because you are also carrying tax burden, unpaid business development, and operating overhead. 

A practical way to build pricing is to create a yearly revenue target and work backward. Add together the owner pay you want, software costs, contractors if any, insurance, taxes, and a cushion for bad debt or downtime. Then divide by a realistic number of billable hours or project slots, not by every hour in the year. That gives you a minimum viable rate. From there, raise prices for projects with more ambiguity, rush timing, more stakeholders, or higher business impact. The SBA’s framework of cost coverage plus market comparison supports this approach, even though the specific assumptions should be yours. 

Do not sell “unlimited revisions.” Do not sell “support included forever.” Do not let every Slack message become free product management. Scope control sources are clear that unclear requirements and unmanaged changes are what turn profitable work into overruns. A better approach is to define a fixed number of feedback rounds, fixed review windows, fixed deliverables, and a written rule that anything outside scope becomes a change order or a new phase. 

Form the business cleanly and be realistic about Delaware
If you want the business to be a real company rather than just a freelance side gig, a single-member LLC is the usual starting point. The SBA explains that an LLC can protect personal assets in many situations, and the IRS explains that a domestic single-member LLC is generally treated as a disregarded entity for federal income tax unless it elects otherwise. That is usually the simplest launch structure for a solo service business. 

The important strategic question is where to form it. Many first-time founders are drawn to Delaware because it is famous, but for a solo services business with no investors yet, Delaware is not always the simplest answer. The Delaware formation benefits are real, but the factual tradeoffs are also real: Delaware requires a registered agent with a physical Delaware address, a Delaware LLC owes a $300 annual tax due by June 1, and if you conduct business in another state you may need foreign qualification there and may owe fees and annual requirements in both states. That is why, as a practical inference from the official rules, many solo service businesses are simpler to form in their home state unless they have a specific Delaware reason such as fundraising plans, legal preference, or a partner requirement. 

If you still choose to form in Delaware, the process is straightforward. The Delaware Division of Corporations says you first choose the entity type, then obtain a registered agent in Delaware, and then file formation documents. For an LLC, Delaware’s official LLC formation document says the Certificate of Formation filing fee is $110. Delaware also allows optional name reservation for 120 days at a $75 fee. 

You should create an operating agreement even if you are the only member. Delaware says operating agreements are not filed with the Secretary of State; they are maintained by the entity. The SBA separately says operating agreements define the company’s financial and functional decisions and are widely recommended even when not legally required. For a solo owner, this document can still be useful because banks, insurers, accountants, or future partners may want to see it, and it forces you to document ownership, authority, and rules. 

After formation, get an EIN directly from the IRS. The IRS says you should form the state entity first and then apply for the EIN. Once you have formation documents and the EIN, open a business bank account. The SBA notes that banks commonly ask for registrations and formation paperwork, and the Delaware formation guidance notes that some financial institutions may want a certificate of good standing or status. 

If your brand name differs from your legal entity name, handle the fictitious or trade name properly. Delaware’s trade-name system changed in 2026: beginning February 2, 2026, the Division of Revenue manages the statewide trade-name registry through Delaware One Stop. Delaware says a trade name must be associated with a valid Delaware business license, and for out-of-state businesses that only need a Delaware trade name there is now a Trade Name–Only License option for $25 per year. If you are actually conducting business in Delaware, Delaware generally requires a state business license, typically starting around $75 for the first location. 

On taxes, the IRS says a single-member LLC usually reports business activity on the owner’s return, often via Schedule C, and a trade or business owner is generally subject to self-employment tax on net earnings. The IRS also says self-employed people often need quarterly estimated tax payments. Delaware’s own tax FAQ says a single-member disregarded entity is treated as a sole proprietorship for Delaware tax purposes, and the Delaware LLC annual tax remains separate from that income-tax treatment. 

If your profits become steady and substantial, you can later discuss an S corporation election with a CPA. The IRS confirms that an eligible entity can elect S-corporation treatment using Form 2553. That option is worth revisiting after your revenue is consistent enough to justify payroll and added compliance, but it is not necessary to start cleanly. 

Do not overlook insurance. The SBA recommends that service businesses consider professional liability insurance for errors and negligence, and it also describes general liability and home-based business coverage. For the type of work you want to do, professional liability is especially relevant because clients can complain about mistakes, delays, unfinished work, or bad recommendations even when no physical damage occurred. 

Finally, clear your name before you invest in branding. The United States Patent and Trademark Office says trademarks protect brand names and logos used with goods or services, provides a trademark database to search, and explains that federal registration is optional but affects the scope of rights. Before buying domains and printing a logo everywhere, search the trademark database and make sure you are not choosing a name that will be hard to protect or likely to conflict. 

One current compliance note is easy to miss. As of the Treasury rule change effective March 26, 2025, the Financial Crimes Enforcement Network says all entities created in the United States are exempt from beneficial ownership information reporting to FinCEN. That means a newly formed domestic LLC currently does not have a BOI filing requirement under the interim final rule, although this is an area worth monitoring over time because federal reporting rules can change. 

Launch in a sequence that protects cash and momentum
The right order for you is: position first, website second, contracts and pricing third, entity and banking fourth, then close the first projects under the new system. That order matters because your website and offer positioning directly affect what kinds of clients you attract, while your legal and financial setup protects you once money starts moving. The worst sequence is the opposite one: forming a company with no message, no sales asset, and no repeatable offer. 

In the near term, your priority list should look like this. Finish your own site with a clear niche, service packages, and at least two detailed case studies. Set up inquiry handling with a short form, a visible email address, and a defined response promise you can actually keep. Turn vague requests into paid discovery instead of free consulting. Use milestone-based billing and written scope on every paid build. Decide whether your real operating state or Delaware is the better formation state for your actual business facts, not for image alone. Then open the bank account, add insurance, and begin asking completed clients for reviews and testimonials. Each of those steps follows the best-practice themes in the official SBA, IRS, Google, LinkedIn, and Delaware guidance cited above. 

Because you are building this alone, use outside guidance where it is free. The SBA says its local resource partners include Small Business Development Centers and SCORE mentors, and that these services can provide counseling, training, and ongoing advice. SCORE mentoring is free and ongoing, and SBDCs provide counseling and training for startups and growth-stage businesses. For a solo founder, that is one of the easiest ways to pressure-test pricing, state-choice questions, marketing, and contracts before expensive mistakes are made. 

The core idea to keep in front of you is simple: your business becomes profitable when it is easy to understand, easy to buy, tightly scoped, professionally documented, and selective about what kinds of work it accepts. If you build your own site around that principle, use discovery to qualify work, package your services cleanly, and choose your company structure based on where you actually operate, you will be building a real studio rather than just selling coding hours.
